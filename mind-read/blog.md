# Blog Post — Target-Specific Guidance

Target format referenced from [SKILL.md](SKILL.md) when `goal` is a **blog post**.

## Interview Dimensions — What to Grill About

After covering the six mandatory dimensions (intent, audience, messages, tone, facts, structure), probe these blog-specific areas. Each dimension includes **why it matters** so the agent can explain *why* it's asking.

### 1. Purpose & Outcome

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| Why are you writing this? What single outcome matters most? | Without a clear goal the post drifts. A post can aim to educate, persuade, entertain, build authority, drive signups, or rank for a keyword — each changes structure and emphasis. |
| Is this part of a content funnel or standalone? | Awareness posts (broad, educational) differ from consideration posts (comparisons, criteria) or conversion posts (bridges to paid offers). The funnel stage determines depth, CTA, and angle. |
| Does this have a deadline or is it evergreen? | Time-sensitive content needs speed over polish. Evergreen content needs comprehensiveness and updateability. |

### 2. Search Intent & Positioning

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| What query would someone type to find this post? | The post must match **search intent** (informational, commercial, navigational, transactional). A post that tries to answer "how to X" but reads like a sales page fails intent. |
| Who else has covered this topic and what will you do differently? | The SERP signals what format and depth Google expects. If top results are listicles, an essay format is at a structural disadvantage. |
| Is the topic narrow enough? | Broad topics are hard to rank and hard to write well. A focused angle ("how to X for Y audience") beats a generic overview. |

### 3. Headline & Hook

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| What's the working headline? | The headline is 80% of the impact. Must be specific, promise a benefit, contain primary keyword, ideally under 60 characters. Test variations. |
| How will you open? What's the hook? | The first 1-3 sentences earn the reader's attention. Options: question the reader relates to, surprising statistic, short anecdote, bold claim, relatable scenario. **Never** start with "In today's digital landscape…" or dictionary definitions. |

### 4. Reader's Experience

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| What will the reader know or be able to do after reading? | State the takeaway up front. The BLUF (bottom line up front) principle: put the key insight first, then support it. |
| Where does the reader's existing knowledge start? | If you over-explain basics the expert bounces. If you assume too much the beginner bounces. Know the starting line. |
| What questions will the reader have while reading? | Anticipate objections, follow-ups, and "why should I care?" moments. Structure sections as answers to implicit reader questions. |

### 5. Structure & Scannability

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| What major sections will the post have? | Headings (H2s) should form a complete outline when read alone — this serves both skimmers and search crawlers. Each H2 covers one idea. |
| What formatting will you use? | Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max), bullet lists for multiple items, numbered steps for processes, bold for key terms (sparingly), pull quotes for impact. 73% of readers skim — structure is retention. |
| Will you include a Quick Answer or TL;DR near the top? | A 2-4 sentence direct answer to the core question, placed early, satisfies both impatient readers and AI answer engines (AEO/GEO). |

### 6. Depth & Originality

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| What makes this post different or better than existing ones? | Thin, generic content fails. The bar: specific examples, original data or frameworks, named sources for claims, actionable steps the reader can take now. E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) determines both rankings and reader trust. |
| What claims need evidence? | Stats, benchmarks, comparisons, causal statements. Push the user for sources: "According to whom?" Flag UNVERIFIED in the plan if they don't know. |
| What's the one insight only *you* can write about this topic? | Personal experience, proprietary data, informed opinion, or a unique framework. This is what makes the post worth writing (and reading). |

### 7. SEO & Discoverability

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| What is the primary keyword or phrase? | Everything downstream depends on this: headline, URL, H2s, introduction (<100 words), image alt text, meta description. Do not guess — research or ask. |
| What related / semantic keywords cover the topic? | Search engines and AI engines use topical breadth to assess relevance. Natural inclusion of synonyms and subtopics beats exact-match repetition. |
| How will the post be promoted? | Writing is half; distribution is the other half. Social sharing, internal links from existing posts, newsletter mention, cross-posts. A post without a distribution plan is a tree falling in an empty forest. |

### 8. Visuals & Media

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| What visuals support the post? | Screenshots, diagrams, images, code blocks, charts — each should **clarify or reinforce** a point, not decorate. Plan what each visual communicates. |
| Does the platform require specific media? | Dev.to expects a cover image and code blocks. LinkedIn posts benefit from a single strong image. Medium supports inline images and pull quotes. |

### 9. Call to Action

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| What should the reader do after finishing? | A post without a CTA is a dead end. Options: read a related post, comment, subscribe, share, sign up, try a tool. The CTA must match the purpose (dimension #1). Be specific — "share this post" is weaker than "tag someone who needs to hear this." |

### 10. Platform & Publication

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| Where will this be published? | Each platform has distinct conventions that change structure, tone, length, formatting, and SEO. See platform table below. |
| Does the publication have a style guide? | If writing for a brand or publication with editorial guidelines, get them early. Adapt tone, formatting, and vocabulary. |

## Common Blog Structures

Offer these templates to the user if they lack a structure preference:

| Structure | Best For |
|-----------|----------|
| **Problem → Solution** | How-to guides, tutorials, technical posts |
| **What? → So What? → Now What?** | Opinion pieces, thought leadership |
| **Listicle** | Curated resources, comparisons, top-N lists |
| **Narrative arc** | Personal essays, origin stories, case studies |
| **Inverted pyramid** | News, announcements, time-sensitive (most important first) |
| **Question → Answer** | FAQ-style, educational content — each H2 is a reader question |
| **Pillar page** | Comprehensive topic overview with internal links to deeper posts |

## Platform Conventions

| Platform | Typical Length | Formatting | SEO | Notes |
|----------|---------------|------------|-----|-------|
| Medium | 800-2000 words | Subheadings, pull quotes, inline images | Tag system, member-only | Algorithm favors claps and reading time |
| Dev.to | 500-2500 words | Code blocks, cover image, tags | Front-matter tags | Technical focus, community-oriented |
| Substack | 1000-3000 words | Email-friendly, conversational | Less important | Paywall gating; subscriber growth is the metric |
| LinkedIn | 300-1500 words | Short paragraphs, no subheadings | Algorithm favors engagement hooks | Hook in first 2 lines (cutoff in feed) |
| Personal blog | Any | Flexible, brand-dependent | SEO-driven — internal links, topic clusters | Owned audience, long-term asset |
| Company blog | 1000-4000 words | Branded formatting, CTAs, structured | Critical — keyword research, SERP analysis | Multiple stakeholders, approval workflow |

## Blog-Specific Edge Cases

| Situation | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| User doesn't know the platform | Ask about their goal (build audience, share knowledge, portfolio). Recommend platform based on content type and goals. |
| User has no hook idea | Offer 3-4 hook templates: question the reader relates to, surprising statistic, short anecdote, bold claim. Let them pick. |
| Topic is time-sensitive | Prioritize speed. Reduce interview depth, get to draft faster. Flag expiration date in the plan. |
| Topic is evergreen | Focus on SEO, comprehensiveness, updateability, and structure that will age well. |
| Writing for a publication with a style guide | Ask for the style guide or link. Adapt structure and tone to match. |
| User has no keyword | Recommend they pick a phrase that matches the core topic and reflects how people search. Use that for headline, URL, and meta. |
| User is stuck on structure | Guide them based on search intent: "For a how-to, a Problem→Solution structure works well. Want to try that?" |
| Multiple possible audiences | Help them choose one primary audience. "Writing for both beginners and experts usually satisfies neither." |

## Output Adjustments

When `goal` is `blog post`:

- **Plan** should include: working headline (with alternatives), hook suggestion, platform-specific formatting notes, primary keyword, SEO meta description line, section breakdown with H2/H3 hierarchy
- **Draft** should include: title, meta description (for the plan), properly nested headings (one H1 = title, H2s for main sections, H3s for subsections), short paragraphs (2-3 sentences), bullet/numbered lists where appropriate, inline links (not footnotes — blog convention), a CTA in the conclusion
- **Avoid**: academic-style footnotes/references, long paragraphs, H1 after the title, passive voice for no reason, preamble/throat-clearing
- **AEO/GEO consideration**: Each major H2 section should open with a 1-3 sentence direct answer to the implied question of that section, before supporting detail
