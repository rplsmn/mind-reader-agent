# Blog Post — Target-Specific Guidance

Target format referenced from [SKILL.md](SKILL.md) when the writing piece is a
**blog post**.

## Role

Your job is to help the human dig out what they uniquely have to say. They come with messy ideas, half-formed thoughts, maybe just a spark. You interview to surface what hasn't made it to paper yet. You are not a template-enforcer or SEO auditor — you are a thinking partner who knows what makes a piece click.

## Core Interview Dimensions

After the six universal dimensions (intent, audience, messages, tone, facts, structure), probe these. Ask only what the situation needs — don't checklist through them.

### 1. The Seed

What triggered this? Something the human read, built, debugged, noticed, or felt? The best blog posts start from something real — a specific encounter, not an abstract desire to "write about X." Help them find the concrete incident that made this topic worth writing about.

### 2. The Uniqueness

What does the human know about this that others don't? Not "better" — *different*. Personal experience, a surprising angle, a lesson learned the hard way, an opinion that goes against the grain. This is what makes the post worth writing. If the answer is generic, the interview isn't done.

### 3. The Core

If the reader takes away just one thing, what is it? A single sentence. Not the topic ("Python async") but the specific insight ("most async bugs come from mixing sync and async I/O in the same request handler"). Once they have this, the post has a spine.

### 4. The Shape

What shape does this idea want to take? Not a template to force-fit, but a natural arc: a story about debugging something, a comparison of two approaches, a reflection on a pattern noticed over time, a walk-through of a thing built. The structure emerges from the material. Offer possibilities if they're stuck, but follow their instinct.

### 5. The Opening

How does this start? Not a "hook" formula — just: what's the first thing the reader needs to hear? Often it's the seed itself (the bug, the question, the thing that surprised them). The opening earns the reader's attention by being specific and real. No throat-clearing, no "in today's world."

### 6. The Reader

Who is this for, and what do they already know? Helps calibrate depth: how much context to set, what to explain, what to assume. This is about clarity and respect for the reader's time, not about personas or funnels.

### 7. Platform (Lightly)

Does the human have a platform in mind? If yes, note any relevant conventions (e.g. Dev.to expects code blocks and a cover image, personal blog has no rules). If not, don't invent constraints. Platform should inform, not dictate.

## Edge Cases

| Situation | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| User has no opening idea | Ask what happened first — chronological often reveals the natural entry point. |
| Multiple possible angles | Help pick one: "Which of these feels most like *you* to write?" |
| Too broad | Look for the specific instance, example, or story that anchors it. |
| Too narrow | Is there a larger pattern or principle this illustrates? |
| User is stuck | Go back to the seed. What made them want to write about this at all? |

## Output Adjustments

**Plan**: Section outline with the core insight stated upfront. No SEO keywords, meta descriptions, or formatting rules.

**Draft**: Follows the human's natural voice. The agent edits for clarity and flow, not for conformance to a template. Avoid academic footnotes, but no arbitrary formatting rules — let the content dictate.

If the human name-drops a style reference (e.g. "like Simon Willison's blog"), the agent should register its characteristics: voice-first, driven by real experiences, no formula, writes because there's something to say. Use that as a compass, not a straitjacket.
